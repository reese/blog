_Nudge_ is a book by University of Chicago professor Richard Thaler and Harvard Law School professor Cass Sunstein, and it focuses on how different "nudges" can be used to help people make better choices about everything from saving for retirement to choosing healthcare plans.
The book aims to defend the idea of _libertarian paternalism_, a term coined by the authors in some of their earlier publications.

The concept of libertarian paternalism suggests that private and public entities should be able to provide suggestions on people's choices without limiting their choices.
The example from the opening of the book illustrates this with children choosing what to eat in the school cafeteria: if you were putting out the food, what would be the most ideal way to present their choices?
Would you put dessert first, which would likely make the cafeteria more money?
Or would it be better to put the healthier foods first, which might improve the students' health at the expense of the cafeteria?
The "choice architect" would have to weigh competing factors and decide how to achieve their goals without limiting choice (by completely removing some options).

Thaler and Sunstein give some more large-scale case studies of this throughout the book, but the one I found most interesting was their option for privatizing Social Security.
The object of their study was this same [policy](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/sweden-partially-privatized-its-social-security-heres-how-it-has-worked-out-2019-09-06) enacted by the Swedish government in the 1990s.
This policy moved a portion of the Swedish pension program into private funds of the individual's choice.
The government provided a "default" choice, but _highly_ recommended that people take a look at their options (of which their are hundreds) and decide for themselves.
The default fund, it should be worth noting, was the product of dozens of industry experts and over the years has consistently outperformed its competitors.
Because the number of options were vast, and because the default was not given higher credence than its competitors, many companies spent tidy sums in advertising, and some funds were able to fudge their numbers to suggest that they were higher performing (when they were, in reality, highly _volatile_).

Furthermore, the Swedish government heavily advertised for people to make their own choices when the program first launched, so many people did, but on average, people who actively chose something besides the default generally got _worse_ returns.
Over the long term, that advertising stopped, and an increasing number of people now choose the default fund instead, and the Swedish government now no longers suggests that people choose a fund on their own.

In retrospect, Thaler and Sunstein largely praise this system but note that a better choice architecture would be to have leaned more heavily on the default, as reducing the number of options would have helped more people in the long run.

But that's the rub: at what point is it a nudge, and when is it a push?
And at what point is a push _necessary_?

For example, if the Swedish government had automatically enrolled every citizen in the default fund and required them to _ask_ to be put in another one, would that no longer be "libertarian," even if it doesn't restrict individual choice?
I wish the authors would have invested a little bit more in a framework for discussion how to decide _when_ and _how_ to nudge.

Sure, most of the ideas in this book are well-proven and seem like good ideas, but in general they seem to have been thought of in retrospect.
Instead, how can we make better decisions _faster_ and more _effectively_?
